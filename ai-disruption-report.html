Executive Summary

This report synthesizes research across 17 domains to model the cascading effects of AI on the global economy over the next decade. Our Monte Carlo v2 simulation ‚Äî featuring regime-switching uncertainty, demand feedback loops, and correlated sampling ‚Äî produces a median estimate of ~21% white-collar task displacement by 2031, with GDP impact near zero in the short term (‚àí0.09% by 2027) as demand destruction offsets productivity gains.

The central finding of v2: AI creates a short-term GDP drag before uncertain long-term gains. By 2031, median net GDP impact is just +0.35% (productivity boost of +4.8% minus demand drag of ‚àí4.3%). By 2036, the range widens dramatically: ‚àí0.7% to +19.8% (P10‚ÄìP90), with a median of +5.2%. This is a story of transition costs and uncertain payoff, not immediate transformation.

The central investment thesis remains: value is migrating from the software application layer down to chips, energy, and physical infrastructure. SaaS faces a ~‚àí18% revenue decline by 2036 while semiconductor and energy sectors surge. The Gini coefficient is projected to increase by 0.042 points by 2031 (rising to 0.077 by 2036). A new bifurcation index captures the growing split between AI-augmented and AI-displaced economic tiers.

Key investment implications: Overweight NVDA, TSM, CEG, data center REITs. Underweight horizontal SaaS, office REITs. Hedge with energy infrastructure and gold for tail-risk scenarios. Watch for the demand feedback loop ‚Äî if displacement outpaces new job creation, the downside scenarios dominate.

Why This Matters

The v1 model showed +9.3% GDP by 2027 ‚Äî implying immediate, massive growth from AI. This was indefensible: the US has never achieved 9% annual GDP growth in peacetime.

The v2 model tells a fundamentally different story: short-term pain, uncertain long-term gain. By 2027, GDP impact is slightly negative (‚àí0.09% P50). The productivity boost doesn't exceed the demand drag until ~2029-2030 in the median case.

This aligns much more closely with Acemoglu's conservative estimates and historical technology adoption patterns (Brynjolfsson's "Productivity J-curve").

‚ö†Ô∏è The Acemoglu Gap: Nobel laureate Daron Acemoglu estimates <0.53% TFP gain over 10 years. Our v2 median shows +0.35% by 2031 (5 years) and +5.2% by 2036 (10 years). The 2031 figure is remarkably close to Acemoglu. The 2036 divergence comes from regime uncertainty: in acceleration scenarios (15% of sims), compounding effects produce outsized gains that pull the median up. In the baseline regime (~50% of sims), 10-year GDP impact is closer to 2-3% ‚Äî still above Acemoglu but not wildly so. The gap reflects model assumptions about the acceleration regime's probability and magnitude.

Cathie Wood predicts a "Goldilocks" scenario: 5% real GDP growth with deflation ‚Äî productivity-driven, not recession-driven. She calls it "deflationary chaos" in the positive sense: goods and services get radically cheaper.

The Bifurcation Problem

ARK's thesis has a critical blind spot: cost deflation is not the same as wage deflation.

  • Cost deflation (good for consumers): AI makes everything cheaper. Healthcare, software, legal services ‚Äî all see price drops.
  • Wage deflation (devastating for workers): The same AI that makes services cheaper also makes the service workers redundant.
  • The paradox: Cheaper goods don't help if you don't have a job to buy them with.

This is where our bifurcation index becomes critical. The AI-augmented tier (engineers who use AI, capital owners, AI companies) benefits from both cheaper inputs AND higher productivity. The AI-displaced tier faces wage collapse while prices in their sectors crater.

üü¢ ARK's Case: "Goldilocks"

  • AI training costs have fallen 75%/year consistently since GPT-3
  • Inference costs dropping 98%/year enables mass deployment
  • Deflationary = good: productivity-driven, not demand-collapse-driven
  • Historical parallel: industrial revolution eventually raised all boats
  • New jobs will emerge that we can't yet imagine
  • 5% real GDP with mild deflation = unprecedented prosperity

üî¥ Acemoglu's Counter: "Not So Fast"

  • Only 4.6% of tasks are truly cost-effectively automatable (NBER)
  • Task productivity ‚â† GDP (transmission rate is ~7%, not 100%)
  • Cost deflation in AI ‚â† economy-wide deflation
  • "Even when AI improves productivity of low-skill workers, this may increase inequality"
  • Previous tech revolutions took 40-60 years to fully play out
  • ARK's 5% GDP implies faster adoption than any technology in history
üîë Key Insight: ARK and Acemoglu may both be right about different parts of the economy. AI-augmented sectors could see ARK-style deflationary abundance while AI-displaced sectors see Acemoglu-style stagnation. Our bifurcation index attempts to measure this divergence: by 2036, P50 is 0.21, suggesting a deeply split economy. The question isn't whether AI creates value ‚Äî it's whether that value reaches the people who lost their jobs to create it.

‚öñÔ∏è The Balanced View

The skeptics don't say AI is worthless. They say: timelines are wrong (decades, not years), economic impact is overstated (0.5% TFP, not transformational), technical limitations persist (hallucinations, no learning), adoption will be slower than hype suggests, and history rhymes‚Äîevery AI boom has been followed by a bust.

Our v2 Monte Carlo results are far more sympathetic to the skeptics than v1 was. The median GDP impact of +0.35% by 2031 is in Acemoglu's ballpark. The Klarna-dip adoption curve embeds Brooks' and Marcus' quality-wall arguments directly into the model. The regime distribution gives ~50% weight to a gradual baseline ‚Äî implying the "moderate outcome" skeptics predict is actually the most likely single path.

The remaining disagreement is about tail upside: our 15% acceleration regime and the 2036 P90 of +19.8% GDP represent outcomes the skeptics consider implausible. Whether those tails are real or fantasy will define the decade.
